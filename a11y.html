Summary

A11y Learning is aimed to help students of design and web development to learn about accessibility so they can create accessible products for everyone.

A11y learning provides quick quizzes and links about accessibility, and an option to create notes about what you have learned.

The outcome of this project highlights the importance of creating web pages and apps that everyone can use.

Problem

Unfortunately, there are still many designers and developers that do not apply accessibility guidelines to their projects for many reasons, such as the many rules they have to learn or a lack of time to study the subject.

On the other hand, everyone has to have the same right to use websites or applications. Something needs to be done to create knowledge and conscious awareness so we can make disabled people’s lives easier when using websites or applications.

Solution

After the research process, I got a clear picture of the target market and understood some student’s reasons for not applying the accessibility guidelines in their projects. From this, A11y Learning was born to help students to learn accessibility consistently using just 10 to 30 minutes per day.

What I learned from this survey is that students who apply or do not apply the accessibility guidelines have the same problems: lack of knowledge, lack of time to study the subject, and lack of time to apply the rules. It is worth noting that 87.9% of survey participants want to work for a company that cares about accessibility, so it is essential to alleviate these problems.

Competitive Analysis

I did a competitive analysis of the competitors: AXE, WCAG Accessibility Checklist, and Contrast Color, to better understand the current market.

AXE for Android is an accessibility analysis tool aimed to help designers and developers to analyze the accessibility issues on Android.

WCAG Accessibility Checklist App is a tool to help developers and designers manage their accessibility checklists. It offers a to-do list with reminders and checklists to help the professionals comply with the three standards of accessibility compliance: Levels A, AA, and AAA.

Color Contrast allows the users to analyze the contrast between a screenshot or a mobile page to see if it is complying with the accessibility guidelines.

Takeaways

What I learned from the Competitive Analyses is that a new competitor could enter into this marketplace by creating a new app about accessibility, but some requirements have to be observed:

  • Clean design
  • Free app
  • Accessible app
  • Tutorial to teach how to use the product
  • Engaging, to have more positive reviews
  • Good marketing campaign to show people how important it is to create accessible products
  • Illustrative of the importance of accessibility to Designers and Developers
  • Application for iOS and Android

Part III: Information Architecture

User Stories

I created many user stories for the product with low, medium, and high importance. I discovered what a minimum viable product or MVP would be for this project. I learned that something essential for users is creating an account, starting the quiz, setting goals, and seeing their achievement. In addition, other necessary features will be daily links to teach the user more about accessibility and note-taking function. At this part of the study, I realized that an MVP would be compounded with at least the features listed below as high importance.

High Priority

  • I want to sign up for an account
  • I want to sign into my account
  • I want to start the Quiz
  • I want to read links about accessibility
  • I want to create notes to remember what I learned
  • I want to update my goals
  • I want to see my achievement

Medium Priority

  • I want to read the tutorial
  • I want to choose the quiz level
  • I want to recover my password

Low Priority

  • I want to update my personal information
  • I want to turn on/off my notifications
  • I want to receive notifications to remember me to learn

Part IV: Branding and Identity

Branding

The next step was the creation of the brand. Analyzing the data collected during the research process, I concluded that an app to help users study accessibility would be an exciting product. It would give users an enjoyable way to make the most of their free time by learning accessibility. It is always good to learn something new, especially if this new skill can help people.

I started the branding process with a brainstorming and sketching session to come up with some ideas for the name and logo.

Name

At that moment, I understood that a name that could be good to represent my brand would be A11y Learning.

I wanted the name of my product to include the word accessibility and also something that explains what the application is about.

First, I considered Accessibility Learning, but this name is too long, so I found a shorter way to express accessibility: A11y, which is divided into A + 11 letters (C-C-E-S-S-I-B-I-L-I-T) + Y.

A + 11 letters (C-C-E-S-S-I-B-I-L-I-T) + Y

Colors

After that, I created a mood board to show the brand’s characteristics and a style guide to document the typography and the color palette that I have chosen for this project.

As a primary color for this project, I have chosen shades of blue with shades of pink as a secondary color. According to the psychology of colors, blue is a color that reflects calm, relaxation, stability, trust, and loyalty. Additionally, pink is a color that is associated with love, kindness, and compassion.

A11y Learning wants users to see the platform as a source of trustworthy support where they can learn a new skill that will transform people’s lives for the better.

Part V: Visual Design

High Fidelity Mockups

The next step in the process was the creation of the high fidelity mockups. After receiving useful feedback on the wireframes, I made some changes to improve usability. For example, I removed one page from the quiz start-up process by adding the course progress to the same page where I display the course levels.

I added some illustrations to the tutorial pages to eliminate the awkward ample white space. In addition, I moved all the buttons to the bottom of the page to show consistency.

Prototypes and Usability Testing

After that, I did the prototypes and conducted usability tests with 3 users: Anuka, Dan, and Erit. I asked them to complete the sign in process using their email, find the tutorial, start the quiz, write a note, use the links feature, change their goals, and view their achievements on the course. They all have experience using applications on a daily basis and gave me very useful feedback.

Anuka was a little confused about the sign in process because I started the test showing the tutorial instead of the landing page. She also said that it would be good to add information about the goals and achievements in the tutorial.

Dan said that the sign in process was a little confusing because I alternated too freely between the phrases “sign in” and “log in”. Also, he suggested that I explain a little more why the questions on the quiz are right or wrong. In addition, he said that maybe it would be better to use the homepage icon in the middle of the menu bar and to have a bigger plus sign to create a note. He said that the homepage was a little unbalanced because I have three items, and it is hard to make three items appear symmetrical.

Erit said it was a little confusing how I presented the tutorial first and the landing page second. She also said that it would be good if I added the ability to choose different goals while testing.

In addition to the critical feedback, they gave me positive feedback on many points. All three users said that the app was a great idea and that I should bring it to life. They also all told me that they loved my design.

I am very grateful to my testers for their encouragement and for helping me find some flaws to iterate the product in the early stages.
