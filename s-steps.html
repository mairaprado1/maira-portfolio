Introduction

I am passionate about accessibility and wanted to make a contribution to the cause in some way. During my time at Google, I had the opportunity to present at an internal UX accessibility conference and to my teams. In those opportunities, I shared some key things I learned about accessibility in my own journey and how we can take small steps towards an accessible world.

Goal

The goal of my work is to raise awareness about accessibility, inspiring more individuals to create products that are accessible to everyone. I wanted to encourage meaningful discussions among my peers, encouraging them to prioritize accessibility in their product development process. Statistically speaking, each of us is likely to experience some form of disability in our lives.

Why we should care about accessibility

  • It is the right thing to do

  • Help businesses expand their reach

  • Minimize risk of legal action

It is the right thing to do

Accessibility is the right thing to do because everyone has the same right to use our products and services, and almost everyone is likely to have a disability at some point in their lives. As Kat Holmes states in the book Mismatch “For better or worse, the people who design the touchpoint of society determine who can participate and who’s left out”. We need to make sure that we design with accessibility in mind to ensure that people are not left out of society.

Help businesses expand their reach

Source: CDC 2023

96% of home pages fail to comply with WCAG 2

Source: WebAIM Million 2023

According to the World Health Organization, as of 2023, there are approximately 1.3 billion people with disabilities worldwide. With a global population of 7 to 8 billion, improving accessibility can lead to increased sales.

In the United States, 26% of the adult population lives with some form of disability, as reported by the CDC in 2023.

The WebAIM Million 2023 report revealed that more than 96% of home pages fail to comply with the WCAG 2, which creates barriers for people with disabilities.

Including people with disabilities can significantly increase sales. In conclusion, creating accessible products can benefit both customers and businesses.

Minimize risk of legal action

There are multiple international laws that have been used to file lawsuits around digital accessibility, such as:

  • The ADA and Section 508 in the US
  • The European Accessibility Act
  • JIS X8341-3 in Japan

Accessibility lawsuits have been on the rise in recent years. According to Usablenet, there were 262 ADA web and application accessibility cases filed in 2016, and that number has risen to over 4,000 in 2022.

This increase in lawsuits is due to the growing awareness of the issue and the recognition of everyone's right to access digital content. It's important to note that regardless of the outcome, accessibility lawsuits can be costly, time-consuming, and can damage a company's reputation.

Introduction to web accessibility

Web accessibility ensures that websites are accessible to all, regardless of their abilities or use of assistive technology.

Before we dive deep into accessibility let’s first understand what disabilities are, considering accessibility is a fundamental aspect of ensuring that everyone can consume content, regardless of their abilities.

What is a disability

The American with Disabilities Act (ADA) defines disability as a “physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities”.

In contrast, the World Health Organization (WHO) defines disability as “an umbrella term, covering impairments, activity limitations, and participation restrictions”.

It is worth pointing out that the ADA definition of disability is based on the medical model (view the disability as a limitation to accomplish some tasks), whereas the WHO definition of disability aligns with the social model (view the disability as a result of the non-accessible environment) of disability.

Types of disabilities

Assistive technologies

Assistive technologies, also called AT, are very important tools because they can enhance the experience of people with disabilities.

AT encompasses a range of tools and software that helps people improve their functional capabilities, such as screen reader, headphones for text to speech, keyboard, braille display, reading glasses, and canes for the blind.

However, to include everyone, independently of their abilities, we need to create products with accessibility in mind. We need to design and develop products that work well if consumed using assistive technology tools.

Designing with accessibility in mind

To clarify, the tips provided below outline some things that can be done to improve accessibility, but it is not an exhaustive list. It is important to understand that there are many more strategies and approaches available to improve accessibility.

It is always beneficial to explore additional resources and consult with experts to ensure a comprehensive understanding of accessibility and its implementation.

Alternative text

Alternative text, also known as alt text, is required for all informative non-text content, such as images. It helps users that rely on screen readers receive information from non-text content. Additionally, alt text is beneficial for users with low data access, as it provides a textual representation when the images cannot be loaded. 

It is important to note that good alt text gives the user the same experience the user would have if they were looking at the visual itself. For instance, if a microphone icon is used to represent ‘search by voice’, the alt text should not simply state ‘microphone’ but rather provide a descriptive alt text such as ‘search by voice’.”

Colors, shapes, and patterns

Do not use colors alone to convey information. It is important to combine colors with shapes and patterns to differentiate information. This is particularly crucial for people who are colorblind, have low vision, or are using a monitor with incorrect color rendering.

By incorporating colors with shapes or patterns we can ensure that everyone can consume the content, regardless of their visual capabilities.

You can check your own designs using the Google Chrome Inspector tool by following these steps:

  1. Right-click on the internet page you want to inspect.
  2. Click “Inspect”.
  3. In the Chrome Inspector panel, click on the three vertical dots on the top-right corner.
  4. Select “More Tools”.
  5. Choose “Rendering”.
  6. Within the “Rendering” options, locate and click on “Emulate vision deficiencies”.

Color contrast

People who have color blindness may have difficulties in perceiving content with low color contrast between the background and foreground. To ensure accessibility, the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) recommend specific contrast ratios based on text size.

  • For normal text (14 points), the recommended contrast ratios are:
    • Level AA: minimum 4.5:1 contrast ratio
    • Level AAA: minimum 7:1 contrast ratio
  • For large text (18 points) the recommended contrast ratios are:
    • Level AA: minimum  3:1  contrast ratio
    • Level AAA: minimum 4.5:1 contrast ratio

By adhering to these guidelines, you can improve the accessibility of your designs for individuals with color blindness. To assist you in checking the color contrast, there are many plugins and websites available. One such tool is the WebAim Contrast Checker provided by Web Accessibility in Mind.

Links

Creating accessible links is very important for users who rely on screen readers to navigate websites. Screen reader users can use the rotor function to navigate through links and gain an understanding of the content, enabling them to click on the link if interested in learning about the subject. However, they can only access this functionality if the website is designed with accessibility in mind. 

To ensure that links are accessible, consider the following tips:

  1. Use meaningful text for the links. The text used for the links should explain what the link is about.
  2. Use unique names. If multiple links on a page have the same name, they should lead users to the same destination to avoid confusion.
  3. Avoid expressions such as “learn more” and “click here”. Screen reader users relying on the rotor function to understand the content of the links would not understand them without clicking on the link.
  4.  Underline the links and don’t underline text that is not a link. Users know that is a common practice to see links underlined so they are likely to be confused if the underlined text is not a link.

Labels

 Labels are required for form fields. Labels are important for several reasons, including:

  • They provide instructions on what to fill in the form fields
  • They can help users to revise the information filled in a long-form
  • They help screen reader users to know what is required

Here are some simples tips to create accessible labels:

  1. Use clear and concise language
  2. Keep consistency among other form fields
  3. Place labels close to the input field

Headings

Headings are HTML tags that follow a hierarchical structure, ranging from h1 to h6. It is important not to skip levels. It is recommended to use h1 for the main heading of the page and then the subsequent headings accordingly, for example, h2 then h3, and so on.

Headings carry semantic meaning and should be used only as intended. If you need to apply specialized styling, consider using classes or other HTML attributes instead. 

It is worth noting that it is possible to use an invisible heading specifically for screen reader users. However, before doing that evaluate if the information hidden maybe would be beneficial also for sighted users. If it would benefit all users, it should be visible to everyone.

How you can help

You can contribute to accessibility by studying the subject, advocating for accessibility, and taking small steps towards creating a more accessible world.

Studying the subject is the first step in the journey and here are some helpful links to get you started:

  • Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG)
  • Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI) Free Online Course
  • WebAim
  • A11y Project
  • Microsoft Inclusive Design
  • Law Office of Lainey Feingold

Advocating for accessibility involves creating groups within your community to discuss about accessibility and brainstorm ways to initiate a change in your surroundings, whether is your neighborhood, workplace, or other environments.

Taking small steps means applying what you’ve learned. Start applying accessibility into your projects, advocating for accessibility and sharing your knowledge with others.
